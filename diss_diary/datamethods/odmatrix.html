OD Matrix Woes

Go back to Data and Methods

May 15, 2026

As I staked my claim to bring in OD matrix data as my primary contribution to this research, the next immediate step is to then source for the OD data. OD data comes in different forms, depending on who produces the data and what do YOU want to use the data for. For example, TfL can use tap-in-tap-out data from all their Underground, Overground and DLR stations to produce OD matrices showing where within the London rail network do people start their journeys and where do they end them. (This is why it is vital for people to tap in! On top of paying the fares to support the system, it will also help PT agencies and researchers to understand travel patterns and make informed decisions on service planning and infrastructure development! But that's a ramble for another time...)

Public transport-derived OD matrices however is not necessarily the best tool for my specific case because EVERYONE takes the public transport for EVERYTHING - school, work, leisure, and more! My research is more on improving accessibility to city centres for WORK purposes, thus a more targeted OD data is needed. The best option in normal situations should have been Census data, where respondents declare where they live and where they work and the Office for National Statistics (ONS) do their statistical processing magic to produce a table aggregated by geographical units (regions, local authorities, MSOAs, etc). The Census also collects information on HOW people travel to work so that one can independently produce estimates of how many people travel from any geographical unit A to any geographical unit B for work on public transport, which is the exact thing that I need!


The Problem with Census 2021 Travel To Work Data
However, the most recent Census in England was done in 2021, specifically March 2021, nowhere near normal from COVID-19. Respondents were asked to report where they worked ON CENSUS DAY (March 21, 2021) except if they were temporarily furloughed, so obviously a substantial number reported that they worked from home. As a result, Census 2021 data is actually only useful when recording the snapshot of pandemic-induced mobility patterns, not when I want to understand mobility patterns in 2026 and propose concrete improvements.


Alternative 1: Mobile Phone OD Data
This is something that, once again, I encountered from the final coursework for CASA0002: Urban Simulation. One of the datasets that we were provided came from a CASA-led research project that used mobile phone data from Locomizer to create OD matrices. Locomizer itself aggregates these mobile phone location data from 200 apps - so essentially it is based on UK-based apps where you guys may have agreed to share your location details while using them or at all times!

Overall, this dataset was derived from a collection of 793,502 active devices in November 2021, so it is about eight months after Census 2021, and from approximately 1% of the English population (as claimed by the paper, see the first assumption below). On top of that, not all of the almost-800k devices were active and provided data over the whole month - only 80% of them have valid location data for at least 14 consecutive days. Some of the big assumptions/drawbacks that I could identify/has been highlighted in this research are:

  • One device for one person - it does not account for situations where an individual may own two or more devices
  • While they do compare the devices' stay locations with actual features on the ground, they also apply a fairly simple rule where the stay location with the longest duration between 2100 and 0700 and happens to be a residential area would be the user's home location, while the stay location with the longest duration between 0800 and 1700 on weekdays and happens to be NOT the user's residential area would be classified as the user's work location instead - it does not account for people with WFH arrangements or non-conventional working hours and does not distinguish between a working adult vs non-working population (students going to school, retirees going to senior centres, unemployed individuals, etc.)
  • The result, which they put up in Zenodo for public use, is the total number of trips in November 2021 based on the data that they have. It is not expanded into the total population for England (since the data is estimated to be only 1%), it does not account for the fact that individuals may take multiple trips per day between the same two locations. The only reason why they could compare it with Census 2021 for data validation is through Pearson correlation, not through looking at raw total flows between locations.
  • The data does not give any indication about the transport mode of the trips between the MSOAs. It just lumps them all together...

Nevertheless, this data has two upsides. Firstly, they noted that, based on identified user residential locations, the data is skewed in favour of areas with higher deprivation indicators, which the research identified as reflecting known socioeconomic biases when dealing with mobile phone data. They did post-stratification adjustment such that the dataset has equal proportions across the deprivation deciles. This indicates that there is some quality control being done with the data that I have. Secondly, this data does isolate home-to-work trips from every other type of trips based on the stay locations that they have identified. This makes it quite close to what I need, which is how many people travel from any geographical unit A to any geographical unit B for work , albeit it is all aggregated and not focused on public transport...
